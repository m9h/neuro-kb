# Cerebrospinal Fluid

Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is a clear, colorless fluid that surrounds the brain and spinal cord within the subarachnoid space. From a neuroimaging perspective, CSF represents the most electrically conductive and optically transparent tissue compartment in the head.

## Physical Properties

### Electrical Properties
- **Conductivity**: 1.79 S/m at body temperature (37°C) 
- **Relative permittivity**: 81.0 (similar to saline)
- **Frequency dependence**: Minimal variation from 10 Hz to 10 kHz

The high conductivity of CSF (comparable to 0.9% saline) makes it a critical component in EEG/MEG forward modeling, as it provides a low-resistance pathway for electrical currents generated by neural activity.

### Optical Properties (800 nm)
- **Absorption coefficient (μₐ)**: 0.0004 mm⁻¹ 
- **Reduced scattering coefficient (μₛ')**: 0.001 mm⁻¹
- **Refractive index**: 1.37

CSF is nearly optically transparent in the near-infrared (650-950 nm), with minimal absorption and scattering compared to other brain tissues. This transparency makes CSF-filled regions appear as "holes" in diffuse optical tomography reconstructions.

### Acoustic Properties
- **Acoustic impedance**: 1.51 MRayl
- **Speed of sound**: ~1500 m/s (similar to water)
- **Density**: 1007 kg/m³

### Composition
CSF is primarily water (99%) with:
- Protein: 0.2-0.4 g/L (much lower than plasma)
- Glucose: 2.8-4.4 mmol/L
- Sodium: ~147 mEq/L
- Chloride: ~113 mEq/L

## Anatomical Distribution

### Ventricular System
- **Lateral ventricles**: Largest CSF compartments in hemispheres
- **Third ventricle**: Midline, between thalami
- **Fourth ventricle**: Between brainstem and cerebellum
- **Cerebral aqueduct**: Connects third and fourth ventricles

### Subarachnoid Space
- Surrounds entire brain and spinal cord
- Thickness: 1-3 mm over convexities, up to 15 mm in cisterns
- **Basal cisterns**: Large CSF collections at skull base

## Neuroimaging Implications

### EEG/MEG Forward Modeling
CSF acts as a high-conductivity layer that:
- **Smears** the scalp potential distribution (volume conduction)
- **Reduces** the amplitude of scalp-recorded signals
- **Alters** the apparent dipole orientation and depth

The ratio of brain-to-CSF conductivity (σ_brain/σ_CSF ≈ 0.33/1.79 ≈ 0.18) determines the magnitude of these effects.

### fNIRS/DOT Imaging
- CSF creates **partial volume effects** in superficial measurements
- **Low scattering** allows deeper photon penetration through CSF-rich regions
- **Minimal hemodynamic response** - CSF does not contain blood vessels
- May appear as **cold spots** in activation maps

### MRI Contrast
- **T1**: Long T1 (~4000 ms) → dark on T1-weighted images
- **T2**: Long T2 (~2000 ms) → bright on T2-weighted images
- **FLAIR**: Suppressed signal → distinguishes from pathological hyperintensity

## Age-Related Changes

### Development
- **Neonates**: Larger relative CSF volume (~15% of intracranial space)
- **Adults**: ~10% of intracranial space
- **Elderly**: Progressive ventricular enlargement (ex vacuo)

### Clinical Implications
- **Hydrocephalus**: Excessive CSF accumulation
- **Atrophy**: Compensatory CSF increase
- **Normal pressure hydrocephalus**: Enlarged ventricles with normal CSF pressure
